A close reading of Graham et al’s “Toward a study of information geographies”

The reading starts off with “Information has always had geography. It is from somewhere; about somewhere; it evolves and is transformed somewhere; it is mediated by networks, infrastructures, and technologies: all of which exist in physical, material places. These geographies of information about places matter because they shape how we are able to find and understand different parts of the world”( Graham, M. p.g80). This entire statement carries a large amount of truth. The paper uses a combination of existing statistics and data that has not been previously analysed to expand on this statement. It explores how information geographies have their own f=geographic distributions, access, participation, and representation. The main point I want to look at is which geographical locations contribute and participate the most on the internet platforms and why this matters.

All information, from academic textbooks to fables and legends are adapted to a variation of local contexts and geographies. This information is vastly mobile in today’s era. This means that this information can reach different people from various geographical locations. The reading states that “ It is precisely this mobility and adaptability of information that provides the motivation to constrain the mutability of information through the creation of what Latour (1986) refers to as ‘immutable mobiles’: or information that can be transported without significant change to its inherent characteristics or meaning.“ (Graham, M. p.g 89) The example referred to is the printing press. The printing press is known to a revolutionary invention but not many people are aware of how much of an influence it would have on the concept of information. The printing press created a cheap method of transporting information while limiting how its form could change. Governments and companies soon adopted the ICT-based non-proximate communication. This was because the ability to create shared understandings of information by non-proximate and asynchronous populations became vital. This led to information and power becoming extremely intertwined.

Now, that tells us how power and information are linked. We haven’t gone deeper into the paper yet, but I want you to think about that statement. Information and power are intertwined- having access or control to information gives you that power. If it still doesn’t make sense, think about universities as an example. In our society, we would view someone with a university degree more highly than someone with just a high school diploma. This is because one has more information- access to information- than the other. This makes them more “powerful” in our society, especially in terms of finance and work opportunity. Now, take that same university student and compare them to the professors who teach them. The professors are viewed at a higher regard because they have more information. The professors could be absolutely inadequate at their job and not display any concrete evidence of their supposed knowledge – but they will still have more power than the average student. This is just a very small and simple example, but it doesn’t cover much of the vital points.

The paper then explains that ‘Information about any location can be abstracted from it and placed in the relatively immutable form of printed paper: thus simultaneously fixing information to a physical object and untethering it to a locale, as this form can be easily moved through space.” This explains how the location ( the geography) of immutable mobiles , such as books and maps, greatly define the location of where this specific location information is referenced from. This mean that something like a book about foods from Japan (an immutable mobile) will end up influencing what readers define and understand about Japan (the geography).

”ICTs have also facilitated an evolution of information beyond immutable mobiles through the creation of ‘(im)mutable augmentations’ characterised by the layering of dynamic information across and over geographic space” This leads to how specific geographies of information, are layered and defined by information where it’s immutability is dependent on who this information is associated with. The immutable augmentations matter because it influences how we understand different geographical locations. They are more than just representations of geographical location- they are part of that location itself. This geography of information has been used to control power for a long time.

The most important part of the paper discusses “ Geographies of access and enablement”. By understanding this, important points on the distribution of technologies and services that are required for digital communication, participation and representations become clear. Internet access is required in order to have most kinds of digital engagement.

It is definitely not a coincidence that majority of the locations with the highest penetration rates have a history of slavery and colonialism. Remember, this paper emphasises that knowledge/ access to information is power, thus internet accessibility and influence means power. Therefore, it’s not surprising that countries that were built off power exploitation have the highest penetration rates.
